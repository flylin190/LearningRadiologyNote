MRI Physics

Nuclear Magnetic Resonance

Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) is based on the principles of nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR). NMR is a phenomenon in which certain atomic nuclei, such as hydrogen protons, can absorb and emit radiofrequency (RF) energy when placed in a strong magnetic field.

Spin and Precession

Protons have an inherent property called spin, which gives them a small magnetic moment. In the absence of an external magnetic field, these spins are randomly oriented. However, when a strong external magnetic field is applied, the spins align either parallel or anti-parallel to the field, with a slight preference for the lower energy parallel state.

The spins precess, or wobble, around the direction of the applied magnetic field at a specific frequency known as the Larmor frequency, which is proportional to the strength of the magnetic field.

Radiofrequency Excitation and Relaxation

In MRI, a brief radiofrequency (RF) pulse is applied at the Larmor frequency, which causes the protons to absorb energy and transition to a higher energy state, temporarily disrupting their alignment with the main magnetic field. This process is known as excitation.

After the RF pulse is turned off, the protons gradually release the absorbed energy and return to their equilibrium state, a process called relaxation. During relaxation, the protons emit RF signals that can be detected and used to create the MRI image.
