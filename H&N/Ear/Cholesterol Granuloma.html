Cholesterol Granuloma

Symptoms:

Cholesterol granulomas are often asymptomatic but can present with symptoms such as hearing loss, tinnitus, or facial nerve weakness if they impinge on nearby structures.

Epidemiology:

Cholesterol granulomas most commonly occur in the middle ear and are often associated with chronic otitis media with effusion. They are more common in males and typically affect adults.

Pathology:

Cholesterol granulomas result from chronic inflammation caused by the presence of cholesterol crystals, usually in response to chronic bleeding and tissue breakdown. The inflammatory response leads to the formation of a granulomatous reaction.

Treatment:

The treatment of cholesterol granulomas depends on their size and location. Small, asymptomatic granulomas may not require treatment. Symptomatic granulomas may be treated with surgical drainage or removal.

Imaging Features:

  • Cholesterol granulomas typically appear as well-defined, cystic lesions with a hyperintense rim on T1-weighted MRI.
  • On CT scan, they often present as hyperdense lesions with central hypodensity.

Differential Diagnosis

  • Cholesteatoma
  • Glomus tumor
  • Epidermoid cyst
