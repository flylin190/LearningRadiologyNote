Gradenigo Syndrome

Symptoms:

The classic triad of Gradenigo syndrome includes:

  • Unilateral facial pain or headache
  • Abducens nerve palsy (lateral rectus muscle palsy causing lateral gaze diplopia)
  • Otitis media (middle ear infection)

    • Epidemiology:

    Gradenigo syndrome is a relatively rare complication of middle ear infections, particularly in the era of antibiotics. It can occur at any age but is more common in children.

    Pathology:

    Gradenigo syndrome is caused by the spread of infection from the middle ear to the apex of the petrous temporal bone, where the abducens nerve (cranial nerve VI) and the trigeminal nerve (cranial nerve V) are located. The inflammation and edema in this region lead to the characteristic symptoms.

    Treatment:

    Treatment typically involves intravenous antibiotics to control the middle ear infection, along with corticosteroids to reduce inflammation and edema around the affected cranial nerves. In severe cases, surgical drainage of the infection may be necessary.

Imaging Features:

  • Mastoiditis or petrositis (inflammation of the petrous apex)
  • Abducens nerve palsy (lateral rectus muscle weakness)
  • Potential intracranial complications (e.g., meningitis, abscess)

Differential Diagnosis:

  • Other causes of abducens nerve palsy (e.g., trauma, neoplasm, aneurysm)
  • Trigeminal neuralgia
  • Cavernous sinus thrombosis
