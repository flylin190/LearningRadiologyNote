Juvenile Angiofibroma

Symptoms:

Juvenile angiofibroma typically presents with nasal obstruction, epistaxis (nosebleeds), facial swelling, and proptosis (bulging of the eye). It mainly affects adolescent males.

Epidemiology:

Juvenile angiofibroma is a rare tumor that almost exclusively occurs in adolescent males. It arises from the pterygopalatine fossa.

Pathology:

Juvenile angiofibroma is a vascular tumor composed of fibrous and vascular tissue. It is typically locally aggressive but non-metastasizing.

Treatment:

Treatment for juvenile angiofibroma usually involves surgical resection. Embolization may be performed before surgery to reduce blood loss.

Imaging Features:

  • Well-defined, enhancing mass centered in the sphenopalatine foramen
  • May erode adjacent bones, such as the maxillary sinus or nasal septum

Differential Diagnosis:

  • Inverted papilloma
  • Angiofibroma in older patients
  • Esthesioneuroblastoma
