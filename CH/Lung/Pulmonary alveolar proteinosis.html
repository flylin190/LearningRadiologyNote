Pulmonary Alveolar Proteinosis

Symptoms:

Pulmonary alveolar proteinosis (PAP) is a rare lung disorder characterized by the accumulation of surfactant-derived lipoproteinaceous material within the alveoli, leading to impaired gas exchange. Symptoms include progressive dyspnea, cough, and fatigue.

Epidemiology:

PAP can occur at any age but most commonly affects adults in their 30s to 50s. It can be primary (idiopathic) or secondary to underlying conditions or exposures.

Pathology:

In PAP, there is an accumulation of periodic acid-Schiff (PAS)-positive lipoproteinaceous material within the alveoli, which is thought to result from impaired clearance of surfactant by alveolar macrophages.

Treatment:

The main treatment for PAP is whole lung lavage, a procedure in which the lungs are flushed with a saline solution to remove the accumulated material. In some cases, lung transplantation may be necessary.

Imaging Features:

  • Bilateral, symmetric, ground-glass opacities on chest X-ray
  • Crazy-paving pattern on chest CT

Differential Diagnosis:

  • Alveolar hemorrhage
  • Interstitial lung disease (e.g., idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis)
  • Pneumocystis pneumonia (PCP)
