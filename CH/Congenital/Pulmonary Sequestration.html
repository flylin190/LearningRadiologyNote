Pulmonary Sequestration

Symptoms:

Most patients with pulmonary sequestration are asymptomatic, but some may present with recurrent respiratory infections, cough, hemoptysis, or chest pain.

Epidemiology:

Pulmonary sequestration is a rare congenital malformation that can occur at any age, but it is most commonly diagnosed in infants and children.

Pathology:

Pulmonary sequestration involves a non-functioning lung tissue that does not communicate with the normal airway and receives its blood supply from the systemic circulation instead of the pulmonary circulation.

Treatment:

Treatment for pulmonary sequestration depends on the patient's symptoms and the size and location of the lesion. Options include observation, surgical resection, or embolization of the feeding artery.

Imaging Features:

  • Chest CT may show a non-communicating lung mass with systemic arterial supply
  • Angiography can demonstrate the abnormal blood supply to the sequestrated lung tissue

Differential Diagnosis

  • Pulmonary arteriovenous malformation (AVM)
  • Pulmonary neoplasm (e.g., bronchogenic carcinoma)
