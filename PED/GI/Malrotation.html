Intestinal Malrotation

Symptoms:

Symptoms of intestinal malrotation can include vomiting, abdominal pain, constipation, and failure to thrive in infants. In severe cases, bowel obstruction or volvulus (twisting of the intestines) may occur.

Epidemiology:

Intestinal malrotation is a congenital anomaly that occurs when the intestines fail to rotate and fixate properly during embryonic development. It is relatively uncommon, occurring in approximately 1 in 500 live births.

Pathology:

In intestinal malrotation, the midgut fails to complete its normal 270-degree counterclockwise rotation around the superior mesenteric artery during fetal development. This can lead to improper positioning and fixation of the intestines, predisposing to bowel obstruction or volvulus.

Treatment:

Treatment for intestinal malrotation typically involves surgical intervention to correct the abnormal positioning of the intestines and prevent complications such as bowel obstruction or volvulus.

Imaging Features:

  • Abnormal positioning of the small bowel and colon
  • Inversed relationship between the superior mesenteric artery and superior mesenteric vein
  • Obstructive or volvulus findings in severe cases

Differential Diagnosis:

  • Midgut volvulus
  • Duodenal atresia
  • Hirschsprung's disease
  • Meconium ileus
