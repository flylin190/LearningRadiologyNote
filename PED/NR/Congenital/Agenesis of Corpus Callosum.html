Dysgenesis of Corpus Callosum

Symptoms:

Symptoms can vary widely depending on the extent of the corpus callosum dysgenesis and the presence of associated brain malformations or conditions. Common symptoms may include seizures, developmental delays, intellectual disability, and impaired motor coordination.

Epidemiology:

The exact prevalence of corpus callosum dysgenesis is unknown, but it is considered a relatively rare condition. It can occur in isolation or in association with other congenital anomalies or genetic disorders.

Pathology:

Dysgenesis of the corpus callosum refers to a group of congenital abnormalities in which the corpus callosum, the largest white matter tract that connects the two cerebral hemispheres, fails to develop properly during fetal brain development.

Treatment:

There is no specific treatment for corpus callosum dysgenesis itself. Management focuses on addressing the associated symptoms and complications, such as seizure control with anti-epileptic medications, physical therapy, occupational therapy, and special education support.

Imaging Features:

  • Complete or partial absence of the corpus callosum
  • Colpocephaly (dilatation of the lateral ventricles)
  • Moose head appearance or Racing car sign
  • Potential associated brain malformations (e.g., neuronal migration disorders)

Differential Diagnosis:

  • Other midline brain malformations
  • Periventricular leukomalacia
  • Acquired corpus callosum lesions (e.g., trauma, tumor, or infarction)
