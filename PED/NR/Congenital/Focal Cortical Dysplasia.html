Focal Cortical Dysplasia

Symptoms:

Focal cortical dysplasia (FCD) is often associated with drug-resistant epilepsy, with seizures being the most common symptom. The severity of symptoms can vary depending on the location and extent of the lesion.

Epidemiology:

FCD is a relatively common cause of intractable epilepsy, particularly in children and young adults. The exact prevalence is unknown but is estimated to be around 20-40% of patients with intractable epilepsy.

Pathology:

FCD is a localized malformation of cortical development, characterized by abnormal neuronal proliferation, migration, and organization within a localized area of the cerebral cortex. It can be classified into three main subtypes based on histopathological features.

Treatment:

Treatment for FCD typically involves antiepileptic medications to control seizures. In cases of drug-resistant epilepsy, surgical resection of the affected cortical region may be considered if it is safely accessible.

Imaging Features:

  • Localized cortical thickening or dysplastic cortical region
  • Abnormal gyral pattern or cortical dyslamination
  • Increased signal intensity on T2-weighted and FLAIR sequences
  • Abnormal cortical-white matter junction or "transmantle" sign

Differential Diagnosis:

  • Low-grade gliomas
  • Tuberous sclerosis complex
  • Ischemic or traumatic cortical scarring
  • Other cortical malformations (e.g., heterotopia, polymicrogyria)
