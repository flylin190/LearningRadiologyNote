Lhermitte-Duclos Disease (ALS)

Symptoms:

Lhermitte-Duclos disease, also known as dysplastic cerebellar gangliocytoma, is a rare benign tumor of the cerebellum. It is characterized by progressive cerebellar dysfunction, including ataxia, headache, and signs of increased intracranial pressure such as nausea, vomiting, and papilledema.

Epidemiology:

Lhermitte-Duclos disease is a rare condition, with only a few hundred cases reported in the medical literature. It can occur at any age, but it most commonly affects adults in their 30s and 40s.

Pathology:

Lhermitte-Duclos disease is characterized by the presence of a hamartomatous lesion in the cerebellum, composed of abnormally large and disorganized ganglion cells. These cells are typically arranged in a "parallel fiber" pattern, giving the lesion a characteristic striated appearance on imaging studies.

Treatment:

The treatment of Lhermitte-Duclos disease typically involves surgical resection of the lesion. This can help relieve symptoms and prevent complications associated with increased intracranial pressure.

Imaging Features:

  • Striated appearance on MRI due to the "parallel fiber" pattern
  • Mass effect on adjacent structures in the posterior fossa
  • Typically hypointense on T1-weighted images and hyperintense on T2-weighted images

Differential Diagnosis:

  • Cerebellar hemangioblastoma
  • Cerebellar pilocytic astrocytoma
  • Cerebellar metastasis
