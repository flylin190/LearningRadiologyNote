Cerebral Amyloid Angiopathy

Symptoms:

Cerebral Amyloid Angiopathy (CAA) is often asymptomatic but can cause symptoms such as headache, confusion, memory loss, and neurological deficits if there is associated intracerebral hemorrhage or ischemic stroke.

Epidemiology:

CAA is more common in older individuals and is often associated with Alzheimer's disease. It is more common in women than men.

Pathology:

CAA is characterized by the deposition of amyloid-beta protein in the walls of small to medium-sized arteries in the brain. This can lead to weakening of the vessel walls and an increased risk of hemorrhage or ischemia.

Treatment:

There is no specific treatment for CAA. Management involves controlling blood pressure and other vascular risk factors to reduce the risk of hemorrhage or ischemic stroke.

Imaging Features:

  • Cerebral microbleeds on gradient-echo MRI
  • Cortical superficial siderosis
  • Leukoaraiosis
  • Microinfarcts
  • Cerebral hemorrhage

Differential Diagnosis:

  • Cerebral vasculitis
  • Hereditary cerebral amyloid angiopathy (HCAA)
  • Primary angiitis of the central nervous system (PACNS)
