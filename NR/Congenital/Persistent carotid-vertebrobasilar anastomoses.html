Persistent Carotid-Vertebrobasilar Anastomoses

Symptoms:

Patients with persistent carotid-vertebrobasilar anastomoses are usually asymptomatic. However, in some cases, they may present with symptoms related to altered cerebral hemodynamics, such as dizziness, vertigo, or syncope.

Epidemiology:

Persistent carotid-vertebrobasilar anastomoses are rare congenital vascular anomalies. They can occur in individuals of any age, but are most commonly detected incidentally in adults.

Pathology:

Persistent carotid-vertebrobasilar anastomoses result from incomplete regression of embryonic arterial connections between the carotid and vertebrobasilar systems. They can take various forms, including persistent trigeminal, otic, or hypoglossal arteries, among others.

Treatment:

Most patients with persistent carotid-vertebrobasilar anastomoses do not require treatment. However, in cases where there is hemodynamic compromise or risk of complications, such as aneurysm formation, surgical or endovascular intervention may be considered.

Imaging Features:

  • Direct communication between the carotid and vertebrobasilar systems
  • Abnormal course of arteries, such as persistent trigeminal, otic, or hypoglossal arteries
  • Visualization of anastomotic vessels on angiography or MR angiography

Differential Diagnosis

  • Vertebrobasilar dolichoectasia
  • Vertebrobasilar fenestration
  • Vertebrobasilar junctional aneurysm
