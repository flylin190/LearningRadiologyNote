Pituitary Adenoma

Symptoms:

Symptoms of pituitary adenomas depend on their size, location, and hormonal secretion. They can cause headaches, visual disturbances, hormonal imbalances (e.g., acromegaly, Cushing's disease, hypopituitarism), and neurological deficits due to mass effect.

Epidemiology:

Pituitary adenomas are relatively common, accounting for approximately 15% of all intracranial neoplasms. They can occur at any age but are most common in adults, particularly between the ages of 30 and 60.

Pathology:

Pituitary adenomas are benign neoplasms arising from the adenohypophyseal cells of the anterior pituitary gland. They can be classified as functional (hormone-secreting) or non-functional (non-secreting) tumors.

Treatment:

Treatment for pituitary adenomas depends on the size, hormonal secretion, and associated symptoms. Options include surgery (transsphenoidal or transcranial resection), medical therapy (dopamine agonists, somatostatin analogs), radiation therapy, or a combination of these approaches.

Imaging Features:

  • Well-defined, sellar or suprasellar mass
  • Variable enhancement patterns on contrast-enhanced imaging
  • Possible compression or displacement of the optic chiasm
  • Invasion of the cavernous sinus in aggressive cases

Differential Diagnosis:

  • Rathke's cleft cyst
  • Craniopharyngioma
  • Meningioma
  • Metastatic lesions
