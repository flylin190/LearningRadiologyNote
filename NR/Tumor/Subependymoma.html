Subependymoma

Symptoms:

Subependymomas are typically slow-growing tumors, and symptoms depend on their location and size. They can cause headaches, seizures, and neurological deficits due to mass effect on surrounding structures.

Epidemiology:

Subependymomas are rare, accounting for approximately 0.7% of all primary brain tumors. They can occur at any age but are most common in adults, with a peak incidence in the fourth and fifth decades of life.

Pathology:

Subependymomas are benign, slow-growing tumors that arise from the ependymal cells lining the ventricular system or central canal of the spinal cord. They are typically well-circumscribed, firm, and may have a cystic component.

Treatment:

Treatment for subependymomas typically involves surgical resection, which can be curative in many cases. Adjuvant radiation therapy may be considered for incompletely resected or high-grade tumors.

Imaging Features:

  • Well-circumscribed, lobulated mass
  • Commonly located in the fourth ventricle or cerebellopontine angle
  • Heterogeneous enhancement on contrast-enhanced imaging
  • Possible cystic or calcified components

Differential Diagnosis:

  • Ependymoma
  • Meningioma
  • Schwannoma
  • Medulloblastoma
