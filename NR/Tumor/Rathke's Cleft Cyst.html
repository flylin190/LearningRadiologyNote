Rathke's Cleft Cyst

Symptoms:

Rathke's cleft cysts are often asymptomatic but can cause headaches, visual disturbances, hormonal imbalances, and pituitary dysfunction due to mass effect on surrounding structures.

Epidemiology:

Rathke's cleft cysts are benign, non-neoplastic lesions that arise from remnants of the Rathke's pouch, an embryonic precursor of the anterior pituitary gland. They are relatively uncommon, accounting for approximately 5-10% of sellar and suprasellar masses.

Pathology:

Rathke's cleft cysts are typically lined by a single layer of cuboidal or columnar epithelium and contain mucoid or proteinaceous fluid. They can be found within the sella turcica, suprasellar region, or both.

Treatment:

Treatment for Rathke's cleft cysts depends on the size and symptoms caused by the lesion. Small, asymptomatic cysts may be monitored with periodic imaging, while larger or symptomatic cysts may require surgical decompression or resection.

Imaging Features:

  • Well-defined, cystic lesion in the sellar or suprasellar region
  • Fluid signal on MRI, often with high protein content
  • Minimal or no enhancement on contrast-enhanced imaging
  • Possible compression or displacement of surrounding structures

Differential Diagnosis:

  • Craniopharyngioma
  • Arachnoid cyst
  • Cystic pituitary adenoma
  • Cystic lesions of the third ventricle
