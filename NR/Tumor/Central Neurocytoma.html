Central Neurocytoma

Symptoms:

Symptoms of central neurocytoma can include headaches, nausea, vomiting, visual disturbances, and neurological deficits such as weakness, numbness, or cognitive impairment, depending on the location and size of the tumor.

Epidemiology:

Central neurocytomas are rare tumors, accounting for approximately 0.1-0.5% of all primary brain tumors. They typically occur in young adults, with a peak incidence in the third decade of life.

Pathology:

Central neurocytomas are well-differentiated, slow-growing tumors of neuronal origin. They typically arise in the lateral ventricles, particularly in the area of the foramen of Monro, and may contain calcifications.

Treatment:

Treatment for central neurocytoma typically involves surgical resection, which can be curative in many cases. Adjuvant radiation therapy may be considered for incompletely resected or high-grade tumors.

Imaging Features:

  • Well-circumscribed, intraventricular mass
  • Commonly located in the lateral ventricles, near the foramen of Monro
  • Homogeneous enhancement on contrast-enhanced imaging
  • Possible calcifications or cystic components

Differential Diagnosis:

  • Ependymoma
  • Choroid plexus papilloma
  • Subependymoma
  • Intraventricular meningioma
