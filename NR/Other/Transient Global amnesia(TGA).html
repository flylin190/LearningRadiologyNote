Transient Global Amnesia (TGA)

Symptoms:

Transient global amnesia (TGA) is characterized by the sudden onset of temporary memory loss, usually lasting for a few hours. During an episode of TGA, individuals experience an inability to form new memories, although they retain memories of their identity and past experiences.

Epidemiology:

TGA is a rare condition, typically affecting individuals over the age of 50. The exact cause of TGA is unknown, but it is believed to be related to a temporary disruption in blood flow to the brain.

Pathology:

The exact pathology of TGA is not well understood. It is thought to be related to a temporary disruption in blood flow to certain areas of the brain, particularly the hippocampus, which plays a key role in memory formation.

Treatment:

There is no specific treatment for TGA, as the condition usually resolves on its own within a few hours. During an episode of TGA, it is important to ensure the safety of the individual and monitor their condition until the memory loss resolves.

Imaging Features:

  • Small punctate regions of abnormally restricted diffusion on DWI/ADC sequences in the CA1 area of the hippocampus (lateral edge of the hippocampal gyrus abutting the temporal horn)
  • No evidence of acute infarction or hemorrhage

Differential Diagnosis

  • Transient ischemic attack (TIA)
  • Stroke
  • Epileptic seizure
  • Migraine
  • Encephalitis
