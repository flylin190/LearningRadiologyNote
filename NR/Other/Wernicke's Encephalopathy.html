Wernicke's Encephalopathy

Symptoms:

The classic triad of symptoms in Wernicke's encephalopathy includes confusion, ataxia (impaired coordination), and ophthalmoplegia (paralysis of eye movements). Other symptoms may include nystagmus, memory loss, and coma in severe cases.

Epidemiology:

Wernicke's encephalopathy is a medical emergency caused by thiamine (vitamin B1) deficiency, most commonly seen in individuals with alcohol use disorder, malnutrition, or prolonged vomiting.

Pathology:

Wernicke's encephalopathy is characterized by hemorrhagic lesions and neuronal loss in specific brain regions, including the thalamus, mammillary bodies, midbrain, and cerebellum, due to thiamine deficiency.

Treatment:

Prompt treatment with intravenous thiamine is essential to prevent permanent brain damage or death. Additional supportive care, such as nutritional support and treatment of underlying conditions, may also be necessary.

Imaging Features:

  • Symmetric signal abnormalities in the thalami, mammillary bodies, and midbrain
  • Contrast enhancement of the affected areas
  • Possible atrophy of the mammillary bodies in chronic cases

Differential Diagnosis:

  • Osmotic demyelination syndrome
  • Infarction or hemorrhage in the thalami or midbrain
  • Metabolic encephalopathies
  • Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease
