Leigh Syndrome

Symptoms:

Leigh Syndrome is a progressive neurological disorder that typically presents in infancy or early childhood. Symptoms may include developmental delay, loss of motor skills, weakness, and respiratory problems.

Epidemiology:

Leigh Syndrome is a rare genetic disorder that affects approximately 1 in 40,000 newborns. It is caused by mutations in mitochondrial DNA or nuclear DNA that affect mitochondrial function.

Pathology:

The pathology of Leigh Syndrome involves bilateral symmetrical necrotic lesions in the brain, particularly in the basal ganglia and brainstem. These lesions result in progressive neurological decline.

Treatment:

There is no cure for Leigh Syndrome, and treatment focuses on managing symptoms and providing supportive care. This may include physical therapy, respiratory support, and medications to control seizures.

Imaging Features:

  • Hyperintense lesions in the basal ganglia and brainstem on T2-weighted MRI
  • Atrophy of the affected brain regions
  • Loss of gray-white matter differentiation

Differential Diagnosis:

  • Infantile neuronal ceroid lipofuscinosis (INCL)
  • Mitochondrial disorders
  • Metabolic disorders
