Freiberg Infarction

Symptoms:

Freiberg infarction typically presents with pain, swelling, and stiffness in the affected joint, usually the second or third metatarsophalangeal joint of the foot.

Epidemiology:

Freiberg infarction is more common in adolescents and young adults, especially females. It is often associated with activities that involve repetitive stress or trauma to the foot.

Pathology:

Freiberg infarction is characterized by avascular necrosis (death of bone tissue due to lack of blood supply) of the metatarsal head, leading to collapse and degenerative changes in the joint.

Treatment:

Treatment for Freiberg infarction aims to relieve pain and improve joint function. This may include rest, pain medication, orthotic devices, physical therapy, and in severe cases, surgery to repair or replace the damaged joint.

Imaging Features:

  • Sclerosis and fragmentation of the metatarsal head on X-ray
  • Joint space narrowing and degenerative changes on X-ray or MRI

Differential Diagnosis

  • Morton's neuroma
  • Metatarsophalangeal joint arthritis
  • Stress fracture
