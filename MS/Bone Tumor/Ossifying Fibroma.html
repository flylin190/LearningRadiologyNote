Ossifying Fibroma

Symptoms:

Ossifying fibromas are usually asymptomatic and are often discovered incidentally on imaging studies performed for other reasons.

Epidemiology:

Ossifying fibromas most commonly occur in young adults, with a peak incidence in the third decade of life. They are more common in females than males.

Pathology:

Ossifying fibromas are benign fibro-osseous lesions that most commonly occur in the mandible and maxilla. They consist of fibrous tissue with varying amounts of calcified material.

Treatment:

Small ossifying fibromas may not require treatment. Larger lesions or those causing symptoms may be treated with surgical excision.

Imaging Features:

  • Well-defined, radiopaque lesion in the mandible or maxilla on X-ray
  • Lesion may exhibit a "ground glass" appearance

Differential Diagnosis:

  • Fibrous dysplasia
  • Odontogenic myxoma
  • Ameloblastoma
