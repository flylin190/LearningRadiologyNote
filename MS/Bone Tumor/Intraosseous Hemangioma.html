Intraosseous Hemangioma

Symptoms:

Intraosseous hemangiomas are usually asymptomatic and are often discovered incidentally on imaging studies. However, they can cause pain, swelling, and pathological fractures in some cases.

Epidemiology:

Intraosseous hemangiomas are rare benign vascular tumors that can occur in any bone, but are most commonly found in the spine and skull.

Pathology:

Intraosseous hemangiomas consist of proliferating vascular channels lined by endothelial cells within the bone marrow spaces.

Treatment:

Asymptomatic intraosseous hemangiomas typically do not require treatment. Symptomatic cases may be treated with surgical excision or embolization.

Imaging Features:

  • Well-defined lytic lesions with striations on plain radiographs
  • "Honeycomb" or "soap bubble" appearance on CT scan
  • Increased vascularity on MRI

Differential Diagnosis:

  • Enchondroma
  • Fibrous dysplasia
  • Osteoblastoma
