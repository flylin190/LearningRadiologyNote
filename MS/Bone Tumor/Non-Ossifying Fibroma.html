Non-ossifying Fibroma

Symptoms:

Non-ossifying fibromas are usually asymptomatic and are often discovered incidentally on imaging studies performed for other reasons.

Epidemiology:

Non-ossifying fibromas are most commonly found in children and adolescents, with a peak incidence in the second decade of life. They are more common in males than females.

Pathology:

Non-ossifying fibromas are benign fibrous lesions that occur in the metaphysis of long bones, most commonly the distal femur and proximal tibia. They are thought to represent a reactive process to minor trauma.

Treatment:

Non-ossifying fibromas typically do not require treatment unless they cause symptoms or are at risk for fracture. In such cases, surgical curettage may be performed.

Imaging Features:

  • Well-defined, lucent lesion in the metaphysis of long bones on X-ray
  • Lesion may exhibit sclerotic borders

Differential Diagnosis:

  • Fibrous dysplasia
  • Enchondroma
  • Simple bone cyst
