Bennett Fracture

Symptoms:

Patients with a Bennett fracture typically experience pain, swelling, and deformity in the affected hand or wrist. They may have difficulty grasping or pinching due to the injury.

Epidemiology:

Bennett fractures are relatively common, accounting for approximately 4% of all hand fractures. They are more prevalent in young, active individuals due to high-energy trauma.

Pathology:

A Bennett fracture is an intra-articular fracture involving the base of the first metacarpal bone, with a characteristic oblique or vertical fracture line and subluxation of the carpometacarpal joint of the thumb.

Treatment:

Treatment for Bennett fractures typically involves closed reduction and immobilization with a thumb spica cast or surgical fixation, depending on the fracture pattern and degree of displacement.

Imaging Features:

  • Fracture line at the base of the first metacarpal, often with an oblique or vertical orientation
  • Subluxation or dislocation of the carpometacarpal joint of the thumb
  • Potential intra-articular extension or comminution

Differential Diagnosis:

  • Rolando fracture
  • Carpometacarpal joint dislocation
  • Boxer's fracture
