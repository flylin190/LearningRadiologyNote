Hepatic Hemangioma

Symptoms:

Hepatic hemangiomas are usually asymptomatic but may cause abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, or a feeling of fullness if they are large enough to compress surrounding structures.

Epidemiology:

Hepatic hemangiomas are the most common benign liver tumors and are more common in women than men. They are often discovered incidentally during imaging studies for other conditions.

Pathology:

Hepatic hemangiomas are composed of clusters of blood vessels that can vary in size. They are typically well-defined and have a characteristic "scalloped" appearance on imaging.

Treatment:

Most hepatic hemangiomas do not require treatment unless they are causing symptoms or are at risk of rupture. In such cases, treatment options may include surgery, embolization, or radiofrequency ablation.

Imaging Features:

  • Well-defined hypervascular lesion on ultrasound
  • Enhancing lesion on CT or MRI with characteristic "phleboliths" (calcifications within the lesion)

Differential Diagnosis

  • Focal nodular hyperplasia (FNH)
  • Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC)
  • Hemangiosarcoma
