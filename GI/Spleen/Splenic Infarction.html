Splenic Infarction

Symptoms:

Splenic infarction can cause left upper quadrant abdominal pain, fever, nausea, and vomiting. In severe cases, it may lead to splenic rupture and internal bleeding.

Epidemiology:

Splenic infarction is relatively uncommon, but can occur in various conditions that predispose to thromboembolism or vascular occlusion, such as hematological disorders, hypercoagulable states, or trauma.

Pathology:

Splenic infarction is caused by a blockage or occlusion of the splenic artery or its branches, leading to ischemia and necrosis of the affected splenic tissue.

Treatment:

Treatment for splenic infarction typically involves supportive care, such as pain management and hydration. In some cases, anticoagulation therapy or surgical intervention (such as splenectomy) may be required.

Imaging Features:

  • Wedge-shaped hypodense or non-enhancing areas within the spleen
  • Splenic enlargement or deformity
  • Potential associated findings like perisplenic fluid or subcapsular hematoma

Differential Diagnosis:

  • Splenic abscess
  • Splenic trauma
  • Splenic neoplasm
