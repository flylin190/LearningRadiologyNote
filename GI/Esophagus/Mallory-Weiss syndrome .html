Mallory-Weiss Syndrome

Symptoms:

Mallory-Weiss syndrome is characterized by upper gastrointestinal bleeding due to a tear in the mucous membrane at the junction of the stomach and esophagus. Symptoms include vomiting blood (hematemesis) and black, tarry stools (melena).

Epidemiology:

Mallory-Weiss syndrome is most commonly seen in people with a history of chronic alcohol use or other conditions that increase intra-abdominal pressure, such as vomiting or coughing.

Pathology:

The tear in Mallory-Weiss syndrome is believed to result from the forceful or prolonged contraction of the muscles in the esophagus and stomach, often due to vomiting or retching.

Treatment:

Treatment for Mallory-Weiss syndrome includes supportive care to stabilize the patient and measures to stop the bleeding, such as endoscopic therapy or surgery in severe cases.

Imaging Features:

  • Endoscopy may reveal longitudinal mucosal lacerations (tears) at the gastroesophageal junction.

Differential Diagnosis

  • Esophageal varices
  • Peptic ulcer disease
  • Esophageal cancer
  • Coagulopathy
