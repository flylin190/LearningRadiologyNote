Pneumoperitoneum

Symptoms:

Symptoms of hollow organ perforation depend on the location of the perforation but may include severe abdominal pain, fever, nausea, vomiting, and peritonitis.

Epidemiology:

Hollow organ perforation can occur in people of all ages but is more common in older adults. It can be caused by a variety of factors, including trauma, infection, and underlying medical conditions.

Pathology:

Hollow organ perforation occurs when there is a hole or tear in the wall of a hollow organ, such as the stomach, small intestine, or colon. This can lead to leakage of the organ's contents into the abdominal cavity, causing inflammation and infection.

Treatment:

Treatment for hollow organ perforation typically involves surgery to repair the perforation and remove any infected tissue. In some cases, antibiotics may also be prescribed to prevent or treat infection.

Imaging Features:

  • Free air under the diaphragm on upright chest X-ray
  • Contrast extravasation on CT scan
  • Perforation site visualization on endoscopy

Differential Diagnosis

  • Acute abdomen
  • Bowel obstruction
  • Perforated peptic ulcer
