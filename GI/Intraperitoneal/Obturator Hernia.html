Obturator Hernia

Symptoms:

Symptoms of an obturator hernia include pain in the groin or medial thigh, a palpable mass, bowel obstruction, and nausea or vomiting in severe cases.

Epidemiology:

Obturator hernias are rare, accounting for approximately 0.5-1% of all hernias. They are more common in elderly, multiparous women and individuals with conditions that increase intra-abdominal pressure.

Pathology:

An obturator hernia occurs when abdominal contents (typically small bowel) protrude through the obturator foramen, a bony opening in the pelvic sidewall.

Treatment:

Treatment typically involves surgical repair, either open or laparoscopic, to reduce the hernia contents and reinforce the obturator foramen with mesh or sutures.

Imaging Features:

  • Soft tissue mass or bowel loop protruding through the obturator foramen on CT or MRI
  • Displacement of adjacent structures (e.g., bladder, pelvic vessels)
  • Bowel obstruction or strangulation in advanced cases

Differential Diagnosis:

  • Other types of groin hernias (e.g., femoral, inguinal)
  • Pelvic masses or tumors
  • Obturator nerve pathology
