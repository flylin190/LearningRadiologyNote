Pericardial Cyst

Symptoms:

Most pericardial cysts are asymptomatic and found incidentally. However, larger cysts may cause chest pain, cough, dyspnea, or palpitations due to compression of adjacent structures.

Epidemiology:

Pericardial cysts are relatively uncommon, accounting for approximately 7% of mediastinal masses. They can occur in individuals of any age but are more common in adults.

Pathology:

Pericardial cysts are benign congenital lesions that arise from the pericardial sac, the membranous sac surrounding the heart. They are lined with a single layer of mesothelial cells and contain fluid.

Treatment:

Asymptomatic and small pericardial cysts may be monitored with periodic imaging. Symptomatic or enlarging cysts may require surgical resection or percutaneous drainage.

Imaging Features:

  • Well-defined, smooth-walled cystic mass on CT or MRI
  • Characteristic location adjacent to the pericardium or heart
  • May be unilocular or multilocular, with or without internal septations

Differential Diagnosis:

  • Other mediastinal cysts (e.g., bronchogenic, thymic, esophageal)
  • Pericardial effusion
  • Mediastinal masses or tumors
